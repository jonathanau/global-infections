🦠 Global Infectious Diseases

Burden, Transmission, and Prevention

Infectious diseases remain among the leading causes of death and disability worldwide. From pneumonia and tuberculosis to malaria and norovirus, understanding how these diseases spread, who they affect, and how to prevent them can save lives.

The Global Burden of Infectious Disease

Infectious diseases sit within GBD's broader communicable, maternal, neonatal, and nutritional (CMNN) category, which accounted for 681 million DALYs in 2023, roughly 24% of all global DALYs. The leading infectious causes below use GBD 2023 all-age, both-sex global DALY counts.

⚠️ Why DALY Rankings Can Shift

GBD 2023 shows how sharply the pandemic distorted earlier rankings: COVID-19 was a leading global cause of health loss in 2021, but it was no longer among the top 25 global DALY causes in 2023. Lower respiratory infections returned as the leading infectious cause of DALYs, while diarrhoeal diseases, HIV/AIDS, tuberculosis, and malaria continued long-term declines in age-standardised DALY rates. Norovirus causes enormous illness volume, but most cases are short-lived, so its DALY ranking is lower than its case count suggests.

10 Major Infectious Disease Profiles

Each disease profile below summarizes cause, transmission, burden, and key prevention strategies using the latest credible global sources available.

🦠

COVID-19

A global pandemic caused by the SARS-CoV-2 virus

Annual IncidenceUnder-reported globally
GBD 2023 RankNot top 25 in DALYs
Reported Deaths~70,000 (2024)
Top PathogenSARS-CoV-2 (RNA Virus)
Most AffectedElderly & immunocompromised
Trend→ Endemic (seasonal waves)

COVID-19 shifted from pandemic shock to persistent risk.

SARS-CoV-2 spreads through shared air and continues to cause acute illness, severe disease in high-risk groups, and post-COVID conditions. Vaccination and population immunity sharply reduced reported mortality after the 2020-2022 emergency phase, but surveillance is weaker now, so reported deaths understate the full burden in many places.

🛡️ Prevention:

  • COVID-19 vaccination & boosters
  • High-quality masking (N95/KN95)
  • Improving ventilation & HEPA filtration
  • Regular testing when symptomatic
  • Isolating while infectious
🫁

Lower Respiratory Infections

Primarily pneumonia and bronchiolitis

DALYs98.7 million (2023)
Deaths2.50 million (2023)
Top PathogenStreptococcus pneumoniae
Most AffectedChildren <5 & adults 70+
Trend↓ DALY rate down 24.8% since 2010

Pneumonia kills more children than any other infectious disease.

Lower respiratory infections (LRI), primarily pneumonia and bronchiolitis, are the highest-burden infectious disease group by deaths and DALYs. In 2023, S. pneumoniae caused about 634,000 LRI deaths. Vaccines like PCV, Hib, influenza, and newer RSV tools have reduced child mortality, but children under 5 and adults over 70 still carry the highest burden.

🛡️ Prevention:

  • Pneumococcal vaccination (PCV13/PCV20)
  • Hib and influenza vaccination
  • Exclusive breastfeeding for first 6 months
  • Reducing indoor air pollution
  • Adequate nutrition

Diarrhoeal Diseases

A leading killer of children under 5 worldwide

Childhood Cases~1.7 billion/year

Key PathogensRotavirus, Norovirus,
E. coli, Shigella, Cholera
Most AffectedChildren <5 (highest mortality)
Trend↓ DALY rate down 49.1% since 2010

A preventable killer of young children.

Diarrhoeal diseases are caused by bacterial, viral, and parasitic pathogens spread through contaminated water, food, and surfaces. WHO identifies diarrhoea as the third leading cause of death among children aged 1-59 months. Rotavirus vaccination, oral rehydration salts, zinc treatment, safe water, sanitation, and handwashing remain the highest-impact interventions.

🛡️ Prevention:

  • Safe drinking water & sanitation (WASH)
  • Rotavirus vaccination
  • Handwashing with soap
  • Oral rehydration therapy (ORT)
  • Safe food handling & cooking
🔴

HIV / AIDS

A four-decade pandemic transformed by treatment

Living with HIV40.8 million (2024)
DALYs45.1 million (2023)
New Infections/Year1.3 million
Deaths/Year~630,000
Most AffectedKey populations; sub-Saharan Africa
Trend↓ Deaths down; prevention stalled

HIV has claimed over 40 million lives since the epidemic began.

Human Immunodeficiency Virus attacks the immune system, progressing to AIDS without treatment. Antiretroviral therapy (ART) transformed HIV from a death sentence into a manageable chronic condition, and 31.6 million people were receiving ART in 2024. Prevention gaps remain: new infections were nearly unchanged from the prior year, and key populations face disproportionate risk.

🛡️ Prevention:

  • ART as prevention (undetectable = untransmittable)
  • PrEP (pre-exposure prophylaxis)
  • Condom use
  • Needle & syringe programs
  • Elimination of mother-to-child transmission

Malaria

An ancient killer spread by mosquitoes

Cases282 million (2024)
DALYs51.6 million (2023)
Deaths610,000 (2024)
Key PathogensPlasmodium falciparum
(deadliest), P. vivax
Most AffectedChildren <5 in sub-Saharan Africa
Trend→ Plateauing; drug resistance growing

Malaria kills a child under 5 every two minutes in Africa.

Malaria is a mosquito-borne parasitic disease transmitted by Anopheles mosquitoes. In 2024, the WHO African Region accounted for about 95% of malaria cases and 95% of deaths worldwide. Drug and insecticide resistance are growing threats, and children under 5 account for roughly three quarters of malaria deaths in the Region.

🛡️ Prevention:

  • Insecticide-treated bed nets (ITNs)
  • Indoor residual spraying (IRS)
  • RTS,S / R21 malaria vaccine
  • Antimalarial prophylaxis for travelers
  • Eliminating standing water breeding sites
🦠

Tuberculosis (TB)

The deadliest single infectious agent

Annual Incidence10.7 million (2024)
DALYs43.6 million (2023)
Deaths1.23 million (2024)
Top BurdenSE Asia (34%), W. Pacific (27%), Africa (25%)
HIV Co-infection5.8% of TB cases (2024)
Trend→ Stalled reduction

TB is the world's leading killer from a single infectious agent.

Tuberculosis is caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis and spreads through the air. In 2024, 10.7 million people fell ill and 1.23 million died, including 150,000 people with HIV. Drug-resistant TB remains a health security threat: only about 2 in 5 people with drug-resistant TB accessed treatment in 2024. The WHO End TB Strategy milestones remain far off track.

🛡️ Prevention:

  • BCG vaccination (children in endemic areas)
  • Early detection & treatment (DOTS strategy)
  • Infection control in healthcare settings
  • Addressing HIV co-infection
  • Improving ventilation in crowded spaces

Hepatitis B & C

A "silent epidemic" destroying livers worldwide

HBV Chronic240 million (2024)
HCV Chronic47 million (2024)
Deaths/Year1.3 million (2024)
TransmissionBlood, bodily fluids,
perinatal

Viral hepatitis is a "silent epidemic"; most don't know they're infected.

Hepatitis B and C cause chronic liver inflammation that can progress to cirrhosis and liver cancer. HBV is vaccine-preventable, yet less than 5% of people living with chronic HBV were receiving treatment in 2024. HCV has no vaccine, but direct-acting antivirals cure most infections. WHO's 2026 report says elimination is still off track without faster testing and treatment scale-up.

🛡️ Prevention:

  • HBV vaccination (routine childhood immunization)
  • Safe injection practices
  • Blood supply screening
  • Harm reduction programs
  • DAA treatment for HCV cure

Influenza (Flu)

The perennial pandemic threat

Annual Cases~1 billion estimated
Severe Cases3–5 million/year
Deaths290,000–650,000/year
Vulnerable GroupsElderly, children,
pregnant women
Pandemic RiskHIGH: antigenic shift
Trend→ Seasonal; pandemic risk persists

Influenza causes the next pandemic, a question of "when," not "if."

Influenza is a highly contagious respiratory illness. Seasonal epidemics peak in winter months, while influenza A viruses can also cause pandemics through antigenic shift. Annual vaccination is the primary defense, but antigenic drift requires updated formulations each year. WHO estimates seasonal influenza causes 3-5 million severe cases and 290,000-650,000 respiratory deaths annually.

🛡️ Prevention:

  • Annual flu vaccination
  • Hand hygiene & respiratory etiquette
  • Stay home when sick
  • Antivirals (oseltamivir, zanamivir)
  • Surface disinfection

Dengue Fever

The fastest-spreading mosquito-borne disease

Estimated Infections100-400 million/year
Reported Cases14.6 million (2024)
Reported Deaths>12,000 (2024)
VectorAedes aegypti & A. albopictus
Regions130+ tropical countries
Trend↑ Rapidly expanding range

Climate change is pushing dengue into new territories.

Dengue is caused by four distinct virus serotypes. Infection with one provides lifelong immunity to that type but can increase severe dengue risk after later infection with another serotype. WHO recorded more than 14.6 million reported cases and more than 12,000 reported deaths in 2024, the highest burden on record. Climate change, urbanization, travel, and Aedes mosquito expansion are widening risk.

🛡️ Prevention:

  • Mosquito bite prevention (repellent, nets, clothing)
  • Eliminate standing water breeding sites
  • Qdenga / Dengvaxia vaccination (endemic areas)
  • Community vector control programs
  • Early case detection & management

Norovirus

The world's #1 cause of acute gastroenteritis

Annual Cases685.0 million worldwide
U.S. Outbreaks/Year~2,500
Deaths/Year~200,000 global
Infectious DoseAs few as 18–1,000 particles
Most AffectedAll ages; severe in <5 & 65+
Virus SurvivalDays to weeks on surfaces
Vaccine?❌ None available

The "stomach flu" that's not actually the flu.

Norovirus is a non-enveloped RNA virus in the Caliciviridae family and the leading cause of acute gastroenteritis worldwide. It spreads through contaminated food or water, person-to-person contact, contaminated surfaces, and aerosolized vomit. Infected people shed billions of viral particles, and a very small dose can cause illness. The virus is resilient on surfaces and resists many common disinfectants, so soap-and-water handwashing and bleach-based cleanup are central controls.

🛡️ Prevention:

  • Wash hands with soap & water (20+ seconds)
  • Stay home when sick (48 hrs after recovery)
  • Cook shellfish thoroughly (145°F+)
  • Chlorine bleach disinfection (1000–5000 ppm)
  • Wash contaminated laundry in hot water

🔑 Key Insight: The Vaccine Gap

Some major infectious burdens have vaccines or preventive tools, but coverage and access gaps remain large. Lower respiratory infections depend on a mix of PCV, Hib, influenza, RSV, and maternal or childhood immunization strategies; diarrhoeal disease prevention still depends heavily on WASH and rotavirus coverage; TB and HIV still lack broadly protective vaccines for all ages. Norovirus, despite causing hundreds of millions of cases annually, still has no approved vaccine.

⚡ Dangerous Co-Infections & Interactions

When two or more infectious diseases overlap in a person or population, the results can be catastrophic. Here are seven well-documented dangerous interactions.

🔴

HIV + Tuberculosis

TB is the #1 cause of death among people living with HIV. HIV weakens the immune system, making latent TB 20–30× more likely to become active. This lethal synergy killed an estimated 150,000 people with HIV in 2024.

→
Lethal synergy
🟡

Malaria + Malnutrition

Malaria causes severe anemia and metabolic stress. Malnourished children are more susceptible to severe malaria, and repeated malaria infections worsen nutritional status, creating a devastating feedback loop.

→
Vicious cycle
🟠

Influenza + Bacterial Pneumonia

Influenza damages the respiratory epithelium, creating openings for S. pneumoniae and S. aureus. Up to 50% of flu-related deaths involve secondary bacterial pneumonia, one of the deadliest co-infections.

→
Secondary infection
🟢

Dengue Serotype Shift

A second infection with a different dengue serotype triggers antibody-dependent enhancement (ADE), dramatically increasing the risk of severe dengue, hemorrhagic fever, and death, especially in children.

→
Enhanced severity
⚪

Norovirus + Dehydration

Norovirus causes rapid fluid loss through vomiting and diarrhea. In vulnerable populations (the elderly, infants, and immunocompromised), severe dehydration can lead to hospitalization and death.

→
~200K global deaths/year
🔵

Hepatitis B + D

Hepatitis D virus (HDV) can only infect people already carrying HBV. Co-infection accelerates liver disease progression, with 70–80% developing cirrhosis compared to 15–30% with HBV alone.

→
Accelerated cirrhosis
🦠

COVID-19 + Influenza

Concurrent infection with COVID-19 and the flu significantly increases the risk of severe pneumonia and death. This "twindemic" risk is highest in winter, putting massive strain on hospital systems and critical care capacity.

→
Severe respiratory distress

🔗 Common Transmission Routes

Understanding how pathogens move is the first step to stopping them. Here are the main transmission routes shared by the world's most common infections.

🌬️

Airborne / Droplets

Coughing, sneezing, talking, and even breathing release pathogen-laden droplets and aerosols. COVID-19, TB, influenza, and pneumonia all exploit this route.

🍽️

Fecal-Oral / Contaminated Food & Water

Pathogens in feces contaminate food, water, and surfaces. Norovirus, cholera, hepatitis A & E, and many diarrheal diseases spread this way.

🩸

Blood & Bodily Fluids

Contact with infected blood, semen, or other fluids transmits HIV, hepatitis B & C. Sharing needles and unsafe transfusions are major risks.

🦟

Vector-Borne (Insects)

Mosquitoes, ticks, and flies carry pathogens between hosts. Malaria, dengue, Zika, and yellow fever all depend on arthropod vectors.

3 out of 4 new infectious diseases in humans come from animals

🏢 Enclosed Environments & Disease Amplification

Every infectious disease on this site is amplified by certain environments. When people share air, water, food, or close contact in enclosed spaces, pathogens find ideal conditions to spread. This isn't unique to any one disease - it's a universal principle of outbreak dynamics.

🏢

Shared Air: Respiratory Diseases

COVID-19, Tuberculosis, influenza, pneumonia. These spread through airborne droplets and aerosols in enclosed, poorly ventilated spaces. COVID-19 and TB thrive in crowded housing. Influenza ripples through offices and schools. Improving ventilation and air filtration is the universal countermeasure.

🍽️

Shared Food & Water: Enteric Diseases

Norovirus, cholera, hepatitis A, diarrhoeal diseases all spread through contaminated food, water, and surfaces.

🛏️

Shared Living Quarters: Contact Diseases

Norovirus, scabies, meningitis, respiratory infections; dormitories, military barracks, nursing homes, refugee camps, and cruise ship cabins force prolonged close contact. Person-to-person transmission is the dominant route for most norovirus outbreaks.

🩸

Inadequate Sanitation: Bloodborne & Waterborne Diseases

HIV, hepatitis B & C, cholera, typhoid. Where sterilization protocols fail, bloodborne and waterborne pathogens spread. Unsafe medical injections remain a leading cause of hepatitis C in low-income countries. Contaminated water drives cholera in conflict zones and after natural disasters.

🦟

Stagnant Water & Tropical Environments: Vector-Borne Diseases

Malaria, dengue, Zika, yellow fever. Standing water in urban slums, construction sites, and flooded areas creates mosquito breeding grounds. Deforestation and climate change are pushing these diseases into new areas, including parts of Europe.

🐭

Rodent-Infested Enclosures: Hantavirus

Hantaviruses. Cabins, sheds, barns, and crawl spaces become risky when infected rodent urine, droppings, saliva, or nesting material is stirred into contaminated air. These viruses can cause severe lung disease in the Americas and kidney and bleeding disease in Europe and Asia. Control rodents, seal entry points, ventilate before cleaning, and wet-clean with disinfectant or bleach; never sweep or vacuum droppings dry.

🫁

Crowded Healthcare Settings: Everything

Hospitals and clinics concentrate every risk factor: shared air, shared surfaces, vulnerable patients, and high pathogen loads. Norovirus, TB, MRSA, flu. Healthcare-associated infections (HAIs) affect 1 in 31 hospital patients in the U.S. annually.

Broader Outbreak Context

📊

Surveillance Bias Is Real

Cruise ships are required to report gastrointestinal illness to the CDC when ≥3% are affected. Most restaurants, schools, and nursing homes have no equivalent mandatory reporting. Outbreaks on land go undetected; at sea, they're caught early, skewing public perception.

🎓

What We've Learned

Studying outbreaks on cruise ships has produced critical public health insights, now used everywhere: better hand hygiene protocols, faster outbreak detection, improved disinfection standards for food service, and the CDC's Vessel Sanitation Program model, adapted for nursing homes and hospitals.

🌍

Every Country, Every Setting

Disease amplification happens everywhere: Ebola in West African funeral rites, MERS in South Korean hospitals, measles in under-vaccinated communities, cholera in Yemeni displacement camps. The principle is universal: enclosed spaces + pathogens + susceptible people = outbreaks.

🔑 Key Takeaway

The risk isn't cruise ships, hospitals, or restaurants specifically; it's any environment where many people share air, water, food, and surfaces in close contact. Understanding these dynamics helps prevent outbreaks in every setting, from schools to nursing homes to office buildings.

🛡️ Universal Prevention Strategies

While each disease has specific countermeasures, these core principles apply broadly across infectious diseases.

🧼

Hand Hygiene: #1 Defense

Wash with soap and warm water for 20+ seconds. The most effective single action against norovirus, diarrheal diseases, flu, and more. Alcohol sanitizers don't kill all pathogens (especially norovirus).

💉

Stay Current on Vaccines

Vaccines exist for flu, pneumonia, hepatitis B, rotavirus, measles, meningitis, TB (BCG), malaria, dengue. Each prevents millions of deaths annually. Check travel vaccines before international trips.

🚫

Stay Home When Sick

Do not prepare food or care for others while symptomatic. Wait 48 hours after symptoms stop. This single action prevents exponential spread in schools, workplaces, and communities.

🧴

Disinfect Surfaces Properly

Use chlorine bleach (1,000–5,000 ppm) or EPA-registered disinfectants. Norovirus can survive standard cleaners. Clean immediately after vomiting or diarrhea incidents.

🥗

Safe Food & Water

Cook meats and shellfish thoroughly. Wash produce. Drink treated water when traveling. Avoid raw oysters. Norovirus, cholera, hepatitis A, and parasites all spread through food and water.

🦟

Vector Control

Use insect repellent, bed nets, and eliminate standing water. Protects against malaria, dengue, Zika, yellow fever, and chikungunya, all transmitted by mosquitoes.

❌ Myths vs. Facts

"Hand sanitizer kills norovirus" Alcohol doesn't reliably kill norovirus. Soap and water are essential.
"COVID-19 is just the flu" ❌ WRONG; COVID-19 is a distinct disease with different severe-risk patterns and post-COVID complications.
"Norovirus is just a cruise ship problem" ❌ WRONG; most outbreaks occur in restaurants, schools, healthcare settings, and other shared spaces.
"You can't get malaria in cities" Urban malaria is increasing, especially in sub-Saharan Africa.
"TB is a thing of the past" ❌ WRONG; 10.7M people fell ill in 2024, and TB remains a leading infectious killer globally.
"Antibiotics treat all infections" ❌ WRONG; antibiotics only work on bacteria, not viruses (flu, norovirus, dengue, HIV) or parasites like malaria.
"Dengue is only in tropical countries" ❌ WRONG; local transmission now occurs in France, Croatia, and Madeira due to climate change.
